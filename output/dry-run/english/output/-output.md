## kakistocracy
---
- **definition:**
  - government run by the worst, least qualified, or most unscrupulous people
  - a state or situation where such people hold power; a scathing label for misrule
- **origin:**
  - from Greek kakistos “worst” (superlative of kakos “bad”) + -kratia “rule, power”
  - formed on the model of words like aristocracy and plutocracy; recorded in English by the 19th century
  - often used in political rhetoric to condemn corruption and incompetence
- **pronunciation:**
  - kak-iss-TAH-kruh-see

## ephemeral
---
- **definition:**
  - lasting a very short time; fleeting or short-lived
  - intended to be temporary or quickly disappearing, like online posts or trends
- **origin:**
  - from Greek "ephēmeros" meaning "lasting a day" (epi "upon" + hēmera "day")
  - entered English via Latin "ephemerus/ephemeralis" and French, first used in medicine for fevers that lasted a day
  - later broadened to mean anything short-lived, from mayflies to pop culture fads
- **pronunciation:**
  - ih-FEM-uh-rul

## serendipity
---
- **definition:**
  - the chance finding of something good or useful when you weren’t looking for it
  - a knack for making happy discoveries by accident
- **origin:**
  - coined in 1754 by English writer Horace Walpole from Serendip, an old name for Sri Lanka
  - inspired by the Persian tale “The Three Princes of Serendip,” whose heroes made lucky discoveries through accidents and cleverness
  - formed with the suffix -ity (“state or quality”); the term later spread widely, especially in science and innovation
- **pronunciation:**
  - seh-ren-DIP-ih-tee

## Bánh cuốn / Bánh ướt
---
- **definition:**
  - vietnamese rice-noodle dishes made from thin, steamed rice batter sheets
  - bánh cuốn: the sheet is rolled around savory fillings; bánh ướt: the sheet is left unrolled and served with toppings
- **origin:**
  - from vietnamese: bánh “cake/rice batter item”; cuốn “rolled/wrapped”; ướt “wet”
  - made by steaming rice batter on cloth over boiling water; technique parallels exist (e.g., cantonese cheung fun), but the names are distinctly vietnamese
  - entered english via vietnamese restaurants after the 1970s; diacritics are often dropped as “banh cuon” and “banh uot”
- **pronunciation:**
  - bahn KWOON / bahn UHT
