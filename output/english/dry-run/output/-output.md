## Kakistocracy
---
- **definition:**
  - government by the worst, least qualified, or most unscrupulous people
  - a political system run by the most incompetent or corrupt
- **etymology:**
  - from greek kakistos “worst” (superlative of kakos “bad”) + -kratia “rule, power”
  - formed in english from greek elements on the model of terms like aristocracy
- **history:**
  - first recorded in english in 1829 in political commentary and satire critiquing governance
  - notably used by james russell lowell in an 1876 letter criticizing u.s. politics, which helped popularize the term
  - saw renewed spikes in media and public discourse during periods of perceived governmental dysfunction, especially around 2016–2018
- **pronunciation:**
  - kak-ih-STAH-kruh-see

## Ephemeral
---
- **definition:**
  - lasting for a very short time; fleeting
  - short-lived in nature or duration (e.g., an ephemeral bloom or stream)
- **etymology:**
  - from ancient greek ephēmeros “lasting a day” (epi “upon” + hēmera “day”)
  - via latin ephemerus/ephemeralis; english formed with the adjective suffix -al from latin -alis
  - related to the noun ephemera, sharing the same greek root
- **history:**
  - first recorded in english in the late 1500s, especially in medical texts for fevers lasting a day
  - by the 17th–18th centuries, broadened to describe short-lived creatures (like mayflies) and brief natural events
  - in the 19th–21st centuries, extended to printed “ephemera” and, more recently, digital content designed to disappear quickly
- **pronunciation:**
  - ih-FEM-er-uhl

## Serendipity
---
- **definition:**
  - the happy accident of finding something valuable or pleasant without looking for it
  - an unexpected but fortunate discovery or outcome
  - a state in which good things happen by chance
- **etymology:**
  - coined in English by Horace Walpole (1754) from Serendip + -ity
  - Serendip is an old name for Sri Lanka, via Arabic Sarandib and Persian Serendip, ultimately from Sanskrit Simhaladvipa (“island of the Sinhala”)
  - formed with the English noun suffix -ity meaning a state or quality
- **history:**
  - 1754: Walpole introduced the word in a letter, inspired by the tale “The Three Princes of Serendip,” whose heroes made discoveries by accident and sagacity
  - 19th century: rare literary appearances; by the early 20th century it entered wider print and dictionaries as accidental scientific discoveries drew attention
  - mid-20th century onward: embraced in science, business, and popular culture to describe fruitful chance; analyzed by sociologists such as Robert K. Merton in studies of research practice
- **pronunciation:**
  - seh-ren-DIP-uh-tee

## Bánh cuốn / Bánh ướt
---
- **definition:**
  - vietnamese steamed rice-batter sheets used as a noodle dish
  - bánh cuốn: thin sheets rolled around minced pork and wood ear mushrooms, served with herbs, fried shallots, and dipping fish sauce
  - bánh ướt: unfilled sheets served flat with toppings (e.g., grilled meats, vietnamese sausage, bean sprouts) and fish sauce
- **etymology:**
  - from vietnamese: bánh (“cake; flour-based food”), cuốn (“rolled”), ướt (“wet”)
  - compound terms in standard vietnamese; adopted into english as culinary loanwords
  - romanized without diacritics in english as “banh cuon” / “banh uot”
- **history:**
  - bánh cuốn is associated with northern vietnam (notably thanh trì, hà nội) as a traditional breakfast and street food made by steaming a rice batter on cloth over boiling water
  - bánh ướt is common in central and southern regions, where the unfilled sheets are paired with regional toppings and herbs
  - both dishes spread internationally with the vietnamese diaspora from the late 20th century, appearing in restaurants and food media across the u.s., france, australia, and beyond
- **pronunciation:**
  - bahn KWOON / bahn UHT
