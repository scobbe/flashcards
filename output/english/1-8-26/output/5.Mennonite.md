## Mennonite
---
- **definition:**
  - A member of a Protestant sect that originated in the early 16th century, known for an emphasis on simplicity, pacifism, and community.
  - Follower of the Christian Anabaptist denomination, which adheres to a set of principles derived from the teachings of Menno Simons (1496–1561).
  - Someone belonging to a group characterized by a commitment to pacifism, simple living, and a separation from mainstream society.
- **etymology:**
  - Derived from the name Menno Simons, a Dutch religious reformer (1496–1561) whose teachings greatly influenced the group.
  - The suffix '-ite' is commonly used in English to denote followers or adherents of a person or movement.
  - Originated from Dutch 'Mennoniet', referring to the followers of Menno Simons.
- **history:**
  - The Mennonite movement began in the 16th century during the Protestant Reformation, emerging from the Anabaptist movement.
  - Originally founded in the Netherlands and northern Germany, the Mennonites faced persecution and thus, many migrated to North America in the late 17th and 18th centuries.
  - Mennonites have maintained a strong cultural identity, often characterized by a commitment to nonviolence and a simple lifestyle.
- **pronunciation:**
  - MEN-uh-nite
