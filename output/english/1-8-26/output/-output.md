## Elle magazine
---
- **definition:**
  - A worldwide lifestyle magazine focusing on fashion, beauty, health, and entertainment.
  - First published in France in 1945.
  - Known for its influential role in the fashion industry and for featuring prominent fashion designers and models.
- **etymology:**
  - French origin: 'Elle' means 'she' or 'her' in French.
  - The name reflects the magazine's target audience of women and its focus on catering to female interests.
- **history:**
  - Founded by Pierre Lazareff and his wife Hélène Gordon Lazareff, it launched as a women's magazine in post-war France.
  - Became one of the world's largest fashion magazines, expanding internationally over the decades.
  - Associated with high-profile models and designers, playing a pivotal role in shaping fashion trends.
- **pronunciation:**
  - EL magaz-EEN

## IRR
---
- **definition:**
  - Initialism for 'Internal Rate of Return': a financial metric used to evaluate the profitability of investments.
  - In medical contexts, can refer to 'Internal Reproductive Radiation', though this usage is less common.
- **etymology:**
  - Derived from finance terminology where 'internal' refers to within the company or investment, and 'rate of return' refers to the earnings from an investment.
  - 'Rate' comes from the Old French 'rat' meaning 'a fixed amount', and 'return' is from Old French 'retourner' meaning 'to turn back'.
- **history:**
  - First used in the context of finance in the early to mid-20th century as financial instruments and methodologies developed.
  - Adopted widely in corporate finance and investment analysis for calculating the potential profitability of new ventures.
- **pronunciation:**
  - eye-AR-AR

## Windsor school
---
- **definition:**
  - A type of school or educational institution located in Windsor, often referencing prestigious institutions such as Eton College.
  - A term sometimes used to denote schools following educational traditions similar to those in the town of Windsor, UK.
- **etymology:**
  - Derived from the name Windsor, a town in Berkshire, England.
  - The name Windsor is from Old English 'Windles-ore,' meaning 'winch by the riverside bank'.
  - The use of 'school' evolved from Old English 'scol,' from Latin 'schola,' meaning 'lecture, discussion, learning'.
- **history:**
  - 'Windsor school' often evokes the connotations of traditional and high-quality education associated with historic schools like Eton College, founded in 1440 in Windsor.
  - The phrase gained informal use more broadly in the 20th century to describe schools or educational systems adopting similar educational principles or prestige.

## Mennonite
---
- **definition:**
  - A member of a Protestant sect that originated in the early 16th century, known for an emphasis on simplicity, pacifism, and community.
  - Follower of the Christian Anabaptist denomination, which adheres to a set of principles derived from the teachings of Menno Simons (1496–1561).
  - Someone belonging to a group characterized by a commitment to pacifism, simple living, and a separation from mainstream society.
- **etymology:**
  - Derived from the name Menno Simons, a Dutch religious reformer (1496–1561) whose teachings greatly influenced the group.
  - The suffix '-ite' is commonly used in English to denote followers or adherents of a person or movement.
  - Originated from Dutch 'Mennoniet', referring to the followers of Menno Simons.
- **history:**
  - The Mennonite movement began in the 16th century during the Protestant Reformation, emerging from the Anabaptist movement.
  - Originally founded in the Netherlands and northern Germany, the Mennonites faced persecution and thus, many migrated to North America in the late 17th and 18th centuries.
  - Mennonites have maintained a strong cultural identity, often characterized by a commitment to nonviolence and a simple lifestyle.
- **pronunciation:**
  - MEN-uh-nite

## Anabaptist
---
- **definition:**
  - A member of a Protestant sect that emerged during the Reformation in the 16th century, advocating for adult baptism only, rejecting infant baptism.
  - Part of a radical movement that emphasized pacifism, separation of church and state, and communal living.
  - Refers more broadly to any Christian group or individual that holds similar beliefs about baptism and church membership.
- **etymology:**
  - Derived from the Greek word 'ana' meaning 'again' and 'baptistēs' meaning 'baptist'.
  - The term literally means 'to baptize again', reflecting their practice of adult baptism even if one was baptized as an infant.
  - The word was first used in a derogatory manner by their opponents in the 16th century.
- **history:**
  - The Anabaptist movement began around 1525 in Zurich, Switzerland, during the Protestant Reformation.
  - Anabaptists faced persecution from both Roman Catholic and Protestant authorities for their radical views on church membership and baptism.
  - Over time, the Anabaptist movement influenced the development of other religious groups, such as the Mennonites and the Amish.
- **pronunciation:**
  - an-uh-BAP-tist
