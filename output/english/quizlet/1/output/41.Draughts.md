## Draughts
---
- **definition:**
  - the board game played on a checkered board with diagonal moves and jumps, called checkers in American English
  - the plural of draught: currents of air or servings/doses of a liquid
- **etymology:**
  - from Middle English draught (also draft), from Old English dragan meaning “to draw, pull”; originally “a drawing/pulling; something drawn”
  - the game name is the plural of draught in the sense “a move” or “a piece to be moved” (compare draughtsman)
  - british spelling retains -augh- (draughts), while American English simplified to draft and uses checkers for the game
- **history:**
  - the game descends from medieval alquerque; in 12th‑century France it moved to the chessboard, and the English name draughts is attested by the 16th century
  - by the 18th–19th centuries, rules for English draughts (on the 8×8 board) were codified; clubs, match play, and printed problem columns flourished in Britain and the U.S.
  - from the 19th century onward, international variants (such as 10×10 international draughts) and world championships emerged, while American usage increasingly favored the name checkers
- **pronunciation:**
  - DRAFTS
