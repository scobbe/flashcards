## Mulling spices
---
- **definition:**
  - a blend of aromatic spices used to flavor heated drinks like wine or apple cider
  - a prepacked sachet or mix of those spices for making mulled beverages, especially around the holidays
- **etymology:**
  - formed as a noun phrase: “mulling” (the activity of heating and spicing) + “spices” (aromatic seasonings)
  - mulling comes from english mull “to heat and flavor (wine, etc.),” attested in the 1600s, likely related to earlier senses meaning “soften” (compare latin mollis “soft” via old french mou/mol)
  - spice comes via old french espice/espices from latin species “kinds; wares; ingredients,” a medieval trade term for aromatics
- **history:**
  - spiced, warmed wines have ancient and medieval roots (roman seasoned wines; medieval europe’s hypocras), establishing the practice of adding aromatics to hot drinks
  - english “mulled wine” appears by the early 17th century; 18th–19th‑century cookbooks (e.g., victorian-era guides) list mulled wine and cider with standard spice mixtures
  - the phrase and product “mulling spices” gained traction in the 20th century with commercially packaged blends, becoming closely tied to winter and christmas traditions in britain and north america
- **pronunciation:**
  - MULL-ing SPY-siz
