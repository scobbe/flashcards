## Challah bread
---
- **definition:**
  - a slightly sweet, egg-enriched Jewish loaf, often braided, served on the Sabbath and festivals
  - in broader american usage, any soft, enriched egg bread similar to the traditional Jewish loaf
- **etymology:**
  - from Hebrew challah ‘loaf; portion of dough set aside as an offering’, borrowed into English via Yiddish khale
  - formed in English as the noun phrase “challah bread,” pairing the loanword with native “bread” (Old English bread ‘piece, morsel; baked loaf’) for clarity
  - the spelling ch reflects the Yiddish/Germanic kh sound; in English it is often simplified to an h sound
- **history:**
  - in Jewish law, separating a portion of dough (challah) is a biblical practice; special loaves for the Sabbath are attested in antiquity
  - the iconic braided form developed among Ashkenazi Jews in Central Europe c. 15th–16th centuries, influenced by German braided breads like Zopf
  - the term entered American English with Eastern European Jewish immigration in the early 1900s and became mainstream in delis and cookbooks by the late 20th century
- **pronunciation:**
  - KHAH-luh bred
