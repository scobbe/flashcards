## Trou normand (calvados)
---
- **definition:**
  - a small serving of Calvados (apple brandy) taken between courses to revive the appetite during a long meal
  - the mid-meal palate-cleanser course itself, often apple sorbet splashed with Calvados
- **etymology:**
  - from French trou normand, literally "Norman hole": trou "hole, gap" + normand "Norman (from Normandy)," referring to the "hole" the drink is said to make in the stomach
  - calvados from French Calvados, the apple brandy named for the Calvados department in Normandy; the term is a toponym applied to the spirit
- **history:**
  - distillation of apple cider in Normandy is recorded in 1553 by Gilles de Gouberville; over time Calvados became a hallmark of regional hospitality and long meals
  - the "trou normand" custom—taking a bracing Calvados between courses—was described in 19th-century France and flourished in Belle Époque restaurants (c. 1870–1914)
  - after the Calvados appellation was formalized in 1942, the practice spread in French gastronomy; by the mid-20th century, sorbet au Calvados was a common version
- **pronunciation:**
  - troo nor-MAHN (KAL-vuh-DOSS)
