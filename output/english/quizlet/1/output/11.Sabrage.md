## Sabrage
---
- **definition:**
  - the act of opening a bottle of champagne (or other sparkling wine) by slicing off the cork and glass lip with a saber
  - a ceremonial performance of that bottle-opening technique at celebrations or in hospitality settings
- **etymology:**
  - from French sabrage, literally “sabring,” formed from sabre “sword” + the action/result suffix -age
  - sabre from French sabre, via German Säbel, ultimately from Hungarian szablya meaning “cutting sword”; related to French verb sabrer “to slash with a sabre”
- **history:**
  - early 1800s: custom linked to Napoleonic hussars who marked victories by decapitating champagne bottles, showcasing cavalry flair
  - by the early 20th century the French term appears in English, especially in accounts of aristocratic dining and hotel rituals
  - in 1986 the Confrérie du Sabre d’Or was founded in France, promoting ceremonial sabrage; the 2000s–2010s saw record attempts and media-fueled resurgence
- **pronunciation:**
  - sah-BRAHZH
