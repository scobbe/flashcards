## Pareto principle
---
- **definition:**
  - a rule of thumb that roughly 80% of outcomes come from about 20% of causes
  - used to prioritize by focusing on the few inputs that produce most of the results
- **etymology:**
  - english eponymic compound formed from the Italian surname Pareto (after Vilfredo Pareto) + principle
  - principle from Latin principium “beginning, foundation,” via Old French and Middle English
  - pareto is an Italian surname originating from the toponym Pareto in Piedmont
- **history:**
  - late 19th–early 20th c.: Vilfredo Pareto observed uneven wealth and land distribution (famously, c. 1906 in Italy), inspiring the Pareto distribution
  - 1940s–1950s: quality expert Joseph M. Juran popularized the “Pareto principle” or “80/20 rule” for defect reduction; the phrase appears in English by the 1950s
  - late 20th c. onward: widely adopted in management, economics, software, and productivity to guide prioritization
- **pronunciation:**
  - puh-RAY-toh PRIN-suh-pul
