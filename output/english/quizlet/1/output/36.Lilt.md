## Lilt
---
- **definition:**
  - a light, lively rhythm in music or movement; a cheerful bounce
  - a pleasant rise-and-fall in speech; a melodious accent
  - to sing, play, or speak with a light, rhythmic swing
- **etymology:**
  - early Scots (16th century); origin uncertain, likely imitative of a light, swinging tune
  - often compared with Scandinavian lull-verbs (e.g., Norwegian lulle “to lull, hum”), suggesting a shared onomatopoeic base
  - possibly influenced by English lull “to soothe with sound,” though no direct descent is proven
- **history:**
  - first attested in Scots in the early 1500s, used for cheerful singing or dancing tunes in ballads and folk music
  - by the 18th–19th centuries it broadened to describe speech patterns, as in “an Irish/Scottish lilt,” during a period of rising interest in regional dialects
  - in the 20th century it became a common figurative term for any buoyant rhythm or upbeat quality in music, poetry, and prose
- **pronunciation:**
  - LILT
