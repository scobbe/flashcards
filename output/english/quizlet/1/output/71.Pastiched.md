## Pastiched
---
- **definition:**
  - simple past and past participle of "pastiche": to imitate the style of another work, artist, or period, often by blending borrowed styles
  - made in the style of something else; composed from a mix of styles (as an adjective)
- **etymology:**
  - borrowed via French pastiche, from Italian pasticcio meaning "pie; hotchpotch, patchwork," literally a mixed pie
  - ultimately from Late Latin pasta "paste, dough" (from Greek pasta "barley porridge"), evoking the idea of a mixture
  - the English verb derives from the noun pastiche; adding -ed forms the simple past/participle "pastiched"
- **history:**
  - in English criticism the term gained prominence in the early 20th century, building on 18th‑century opera "pasticcios" assembled from various composers
  - the verb form "pastiched" is attested by the mid‑20th century and spread in film, architecture, and literature to label deliberate stylistic imitation
  - usage grew in the 1970s–1990s with postmodernism; critics such as Fredric Jameson (1984) contrasted "pastiche" with parody in cultural analysis
- **pronunciation:**
  - pas-TEESHT
