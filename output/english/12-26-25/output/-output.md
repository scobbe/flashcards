## Kakistocracy
---
- **definition:**
  - a government run by the least qualified or most unscrupulous people
  - a situation where the worst individuals end up in power; often used as a sharp critique of a regime
- **origin:**
  - from Greek kakistos “worst” (superlative of kakos “bad”) + kratos “rule, power”
  - first recorded in English in 1829; popular in 19th‑century political writing (e.g., James Russell Lowell) to denounce corrupt rule
  - modeled on words like aristocracy and plutocracy, but flipped to mean “rule of the worst”
- **pronunciation:**
  - kak-uh-STAH-kruh-see

## Parapet vs rampart
---
- **definition:**
  - parapet: a low protective wall along the edge of a roof, balcony, bridge, or atop a fortification
  - rampart: a broad defensive embankment or wall, often an earthwork surrounding a fort and typically topped by a parapet
  - difference: a parapet is the breast-high barrier at the top; a rampart is the large defensive mound or wall beneath it
- **origin:**
  - parapet comes via French parapet from Italian parapetto, from parare "to ward off" + petto "breast, chest" (Latin pectus), literally a "breast-high defense"
  - rampart comes from Middle French rempart "defensive wall," from remparer "to fortify, protect," ultimately from Latin parare "to prepare, ward off"
  - in fortification history, the rampart is the main earthwork around a fort, while the parapet is the protective breastwork on top for defenders to fire from
- **pronunciation:**
  - parapet: PAIR-uh-pet; rampart: RAM-part
