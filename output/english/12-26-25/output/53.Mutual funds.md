## Mutual funds
---
- **definition:**
  - a pooled investment that collects money from many people to buy a mix of stocks, bonds, or other assets managed by professionals
  - a share in such a pool that gives each investor proportional ownership and returns
- **etymology:**
  - from mutual (via Middle French, from Latin mutuus “reciprocal, borrowed”) + fund (via French fonds, from Latin fundus “bottom, foundation; resources”), forming an English compound
  - the phrase describes money held in common; mutual funds is simply the plural of the English compound mutual fund
- **history:**
  - pooled investment vehicles appeared in the 18th century, notably Dutch investment trusts (1774); the modern open‑end mutual fund began with Massachusetts Investors Trust in 1924
  - the U.S. Investment Company Act of 1940 set rules for mutual funds, enabling wider public participation after World War II
  - index mutual funds launched in 1976 under John C. Bogle at Vanguard; the spread of 401(k) plans in the 1980s–90s made mutual funds a core household investment
- **pronunciation:**
  - MYOO-choo-uhl FUNDS
