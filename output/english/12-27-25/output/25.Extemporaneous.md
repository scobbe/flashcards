## Extemporaneous
---
- **definition:**
  - spoken or performed without preparation; improvised
  - prepared only briefly or with notes and delivered without a full script
  - made quickly for immediate use; makeshift
- **etymology:**
  - from Latin extemporaneus, meaning "on the spur of the moment," derived from the phrase ex tempore "out of/at the time"
  - built from ex- "out of, from" + tempus/tempor- "time," with the adjective ending -ous added in English via learned Latin formation
  - entered English in the 17th century through scholarly use of Neo-Latin
- **history:**
  - first recorded in English in the early 1600s (17th century), reflecting a period of heavy borrowing from Latin in scholarly and rhetorical writing
  - by the 18thâ€“19th centuries, it became central in oratory and preaching; revivalist leaders like Charles G. Finney championed extemporaneous sermons
  - in the 20th century, "extemporaneous speaking" became a formal event in academic competitions, cementing the term in speech education
- **pronunciation:**
  - ek-stem-puh-RAY-nee-uhs
