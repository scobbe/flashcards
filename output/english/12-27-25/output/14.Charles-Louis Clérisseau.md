## Charles-Louis Clérisseau
---
- **definition:**
  - a French architect, draughtsman, and antiquarian famed for meticulous drawings of ancient Roman ruins
  - a leading promoter of early Neoclassicism who advised figures such as Robert Adam, Catherine the Great, and Thomas Jefferson
- **etymology:**
  - a French compound given name (Charles + Louis) plus the surname Clérisseau
  - charles from Old French Charles < Germanic Karl "free man"
  - louis from Old French Louis < Frankish/Old High German Hludwig "famous warrior"; clérisseau from French clerc "cleric" (Latin clericus) with the suffix -eau, literally "(little) cleric"
- **history:**
  - born 1721 in Paris; trained at the French Academy in Rome in the 1740s amid Grand Tour enthusiasm for antiquity
  - in the 1750s worked with Robert Adam; his drawings fed into Adam's The Ruins of the Palace of the Emperor Diocletian at Spalatro (1764), shaping the Adam style
  - published Monumens de Nismes (1778); in 1785 advised Thomas Jefferson on the Virginia State Capitol; also enjoyed patronage from Catherine the Great; died 1820
- **pronunciation:**
  - SHARL loo-EE klay-ree-SOH
