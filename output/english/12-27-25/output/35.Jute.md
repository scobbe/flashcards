## Jute
---
- **definition:**
  - a coarse, strong plant fiber used for burlap, sacks, rope, and twine
  - the tropical plant (genus Corchorus) that produces this fiber
  - a member of the early Germanic people (the Jutes) who settled parts of Britain
- **etymology:**
  - from Bengali jhut/jhūṭ meaning “coarse, matted fiber,” probably related to Sanskrit jaṭā “matted hair”
  - the tribal name Jute comes via Old English (Ēotas) and Old Norse (Jótar), denoting the people of Jutland
- **history:**
  - entered English in the 1740s through British trade in Bengal; Dundee, Scotland mechanized jute spinning in the 1830s; the first Indian jute mill opened near Calcutta in 1855
  - supplied essential sacks and sandbags in World Wars I and II; after the 1947 Partition, raw jute was largely grown in East Pakistan (now Bangladesh) while many mills remained in India
  - the Jutes, from the Jutland peninsula, migrated to Britain in the 5th–6th centuries; Bede (c. 731) records their settlement in Kent and the Isle of Wight
- **pronunciation:**
  - JOOT
