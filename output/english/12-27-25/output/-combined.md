## ABV
---
- **definition:**
  - a standard measure of how much alcohol (ethanol) is in a drink, expressed as a percentage of the total volume
  - used on labels to compare the strength of beers, wines, and spirits (for example, 5% abv)
- **etymology:**
  - formed in English as an initialism of "alcohol by volume"
  - alcohol from Arabic al-kuhl ("the kohl"), via Medieval Latin alcohol, broadened to mean distilled spirit
  - volume from Latin volumen ("roll, scroll"), later "bulk, size"; by from Old English bi ("near; by means of")
- **history:**
  - mid-20th century: adopted in scientific and regulatory contexts as a metric alternative to "proof," with measurement standardized at 20°C
  - 1987: the European Community required alcoholic strength "by volume" on most beverage labels, accelerating global adoption of ABV
  - 1990s–present: consumer use grew with the craft beer and spirits boom; in the US, ABV commonly appears alongside "proof" (proof = 2× ABV)
- **pronunciation:**
  - ay-BEE-vee

## Addled
---
- **definition:**
  - confused or unable to think clearly; muddled
  - rotten or spoiled, especially said of eggs
- **etymology:**
  - from Middle English adel/addle meaning 'foul, putrid,' ultimately from Old English adela meaning 'filth, mire'
  - formed as the past participle of the obsolete verb addle 'to make or become foul/rotten,' hence 'addled' = 'made foul'
  - unrelated to the obsolete verb addle meaning 'to earn,' which has a different origin
- **history:**
  - first recorded in English c. 1500 describing spoiled or non-developing eggs, a common concern in household and farm life
  - by the late 1500s (1590s) it developed the figurative sense 'muddled in mind,' yielding phrases like 'addled brains' or 'addled wits'
  - remained common in 17th–19th‑century writing and speech; today chiefly informal, often used humorously or mildly critically
- **pronunciation:**
  - AD-uhld

## Albert Schweitzer
---
- **definition:**
  - a theologian, physician, and humanitarian known for founding a hospital at Lambaréné (Gabon) and for his ethic of “reverence for life”
  - an acclaimed organist and Bach scholar who wrote influential studies on Johann Sebastian Bach
- **etymology:**
  - from German given name Albert, ultimately from Old High German adal (“noble”) + beraht (“bright, famous”)
  - surname Schweitzer is German for “Swiss person,” formed from Schweiz (“Switzerland”) + -er (“one from; inhabitant of”)
  - the full name entered English directly from German with its original spelling, the “tz” representing a “ts” sound
- **history:**
  - born 1875 in Alsace; by 1905–06 published major works on Bach and historical Jesus; retrained in medicine and in 1913 opened a mission hospital at Lambaréné in French Equatorial Africa
  - interned during World War I as a German national; rebuilt and expanded the hospital from 1924; awarded the 1952 Nobel Peace Prize (Nobel lecture delivered in 1954, “The Problem of Peace”)
  - in 1957 issued his anti–nuclear weapons appeal “Declaration of Conscience”; widely celebrated mid‑century, later critiqued for paternalism, yet remains a symbol of medical humanitarianism and ethical activism; died 1965
- **pronunciation:**
  - AL-bert SHVYTE-ser

## Assaying
---
- **definition:**
  - testing and measuring a metal, ore, or other sample to determine its components or quality

  - from Middle English assaien/assayen “to try, test,” via Anglo-Norman/Old French essai “trial” and essayer “to try”

  - attested in English by the late 13th–early 14th century meaning “to try or test,” amid growing craft and legal quality controls

  - in 19th-century gold and silver rushes assaying became central to mining and minting; in the 20th century it broadened to standardized chemical and bioassays

## Bertrand Russell
---
- **definition:**
  - british philosopher, logician, and public intellectual (1872–1970)
  - co-author of Principia Mathematica; central figure in analytic philosophy and modern mathematical logic
  - social critic and activist for peace and civil liberties; Nobel Prize in Literature (1950)

  - bertrand: from Old French Bertrand, from Germanic berht (“bright, famous”) + rand (“rim, shield-edge”)
  - russell: from Anglo-Norman Roussel, diminutive of Old French rous (“red[-haired]”); literally “little red one”
  - the full name pairs a French-derived given name with a Norman French surname long naturalized in English
- **history:**
  - born 1872 into a prominent British political family; formulated Russell’s paradox (1901) and, with A. N. Whitehead, published Principia Mathematica (1910–1913), reshaping logic and analytic philosophy
  - opposed conscription in World War I, lost his Cambridge post (1916), and was jailed in 1918; later co-authored the 1955 Russell–Einstein Manifesto, helping catalyze the Pugwash Conferences on science and world affairs
  - awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1950; remained a leading public intellectual through the 1960s, advocating nuclear disarmament and civil liberties until his death in 1970
- **pronunciation:**
  - BUR-trund RUSS-uhl

  - widely popularized in the 1920s by U.S. President Warren G. Harding (1921–23), who joked about his own “bloviating” and was known for windy speeches
  - since the late 20th century it has been common in political commentary to criticize verbose, substance-light talk

## Emphysema
---
- **definition:**
  - a chronic lung disease where the tiny air sacs (alveoli) are destroyed, trapping air and making breathing hard
  - the abnormal presence of air in tissues (such as under the skin) due to injury or infection

  - from Greek emphysēma meaning "inflation, a swelling by blowing," from emphysan "to inflate" (en "in" + physa "bellows; breath")

  - built with the Greek suffix -ēma, forming nouns for the result of an action
- **history:**
  - first recorded in English medical texts in the 1660s, initially for air trapped in tissues after wounds or infections, noted by surgeons and anatomists
  - in 1819 René Laennec used the newly invented stethoscope to describe pulmonary emphysema and differentiate chest diseases during the rise of clinical pathology
  - mid-20th-century studies tied cigarette smoking and urban pollution to chronic bronchitis and emphysema; by the 1960s the term COPD spread, reinforced by reports like the 1964 U.S. Surgeon General’s
- **pronunciation:**
  - em-fuh-SEE-muh

## Eratosthenes

- **history:**

  - in the Hellenistic period he advanced geography (coining the term and mapping the known world), astronomy (estimating Earth’s axial tilt), and mathematics (the prime-number sieve), influencing later figures like Strabo and Ptolemy
  - his name reemerged prominently in European learning during the Renaissance (15th–16th centuries); by the 19th century English texts widely referred to the algorithm as the “sieve of Eratosthenes”
- **pronunciation:**
  - air-uh-TOS-thuh-neez

## Vesak
---

  - the lunar month (Vaisakha/Vesak) in South Asian calendars during which the festival falls
- **etymology:**
  - from Pali vesākha and Sanskrit vaiśākha, “of Viśākhā,” the lunar mansion/asterism (literally ‘forked/branched’)
  - entered English via Sinhala Vesak/Wesak; spelling reflects adaptation from Pali/Sanskrit month-name into local usage
- **history:**
  - observed since ancient India as Vesākha Pūrṇimā; full‑moon rites (uposatha) gained wide support by the 3rd century BCE under Mauryan patronage, including Ashoka
  - the World Fellowship of Buddhists in 1950 endorsed Vesak to mark the Buddha’s birth; in 1999 the UN recognized the International Day of Vesak
  - the term appears in English from the late 19th century (often as Wesak) in accounts of British Ceylon; modern usage favors Vesak, especially in Theravada countries
- **pronunciation:**
  - VAY-sahk

## Vision Quest
---
- **definition:**
  - a rite of passage in some Indigenous North American cultures where a person fasts and prays alone to seek a guiding vision
  - any personal journey undertaken to find purpose, direction, or insight, often through solitude and challenge
  - informally, a determined pursuit of a goal that someone feels called to achieve
- **etymology:**
  - english compound of vision + quest, literally "seeing" + "search"
  - vision from Latin visio "act of seeing," from videre "to see," via Old French and Middle English
  - quest from Old French queste "search, inquiry," from Latin quaerere "to seek, ask," entering Middle English as quest(e)
- **history:**
  - the practice is ancient among many Indigenous nations; the English phrase appears in print by the early 20th century in ethnographic accounts
  - black Elk Speaks (1932) and mid-20th-century anthropology popularized the concept for non-Native readers, shaping how the rite was framed
  - from the 1960s-1980s the term spread into counterculture, self-help, and pop culture (e.g., the 1985 film), and since then debates over misuse and cultural appropriation have intensified
- **pronunciation:**
  - VI-zhun kwest

## Yalda
---
- **definition:**
  - the Iranian winter-solstice festival marking the year's longest night, also called Shab-e Yalda or Chelleh Night
  - a female given name of Persian origin inspired by the festival
- **etymology:**
  - from Classical Syriac yaldā, meaning 'birth,' built on the Semitic root y‑l‑d 'to give birth'; adopted into Persian via early Christian communities
  - used in Persian as shab‑e yalda ('night of birth'), referring to the sun's symbolic 'birth' at the solstice; often paired in usage with the native term chelleh ('forty')
- **history:**
  - pre‑Islamic Iranians marked the winter solstice as the turning point toward longer days; under the Sasanian Empire (3rd–7th c. CE), the Syriac term yaldā circulated among Christian communities
  - in medieval Persian literature (13th–15th c.), poets such as Hafez used shab‑e yalda as a metaphor for the darkest night before dawn
  - the word entered English by the early 20th century via travel and scholarly writings; in 2022, 'Yalda/Chella' was inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list (Iran and Afghanistan)
- **pronunciation:**
  - yahl-DAH
