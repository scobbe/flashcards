## Bertrand Russell
---
- **definition:**
  - british philosopher, logician, and public intellectual (1872–1970)
  - co-author of Principia Mathematica; central figure in analytic philosophy and modern mathematical logic
  - social critic and activist for peace and civil liberties; Nobel Prize in Literature (1950)
- **etymology:**
  - bertrand: from Old French Bertrand, from Germanic berht (“bright, famous”) + rand (“rim, shield-edge”)
  - russell: from Anglo-Norman Roussel, diminutive of Old French rous (“red[-haired]”); literally “little red one”
  - the full name pairs a French-derived given name with a Norman French surname long naturalized in English
- **history:**
  - born 1872 into a prominent British political family; formulated Russell’s paradox (1901) and, with A. N. Whitehead, published Principia Mathematica (1910–1913), reshaping logic and analytic philosophy
  - opposed conscription in World War I, lost his Cambridge post (1916), and was jailed in 1918; later co-authored the 1955 Russell–Einstein Manifesto, helping catalyze the Pugwash Conferences on science and world affairs
  - awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1950; remained a leading public intellectual through the 1960s, advocating nuclear disarmament and civil liberties until his death in 1970
- **pronunciation:**
  - BUR-trund RUSS-uhl
