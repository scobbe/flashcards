## children of officials
### guān’èrdài
---
- **definition:** children of officials
- **pinyin:** guān’èrdài
- **components:**
  - 官
    - guān
    - official
  - 二
    - èr
    - two; second
  - 代
    - dài
    - generation
- **etymology:**
  - **type:** compound word
  - **description:** 官 (guān, "official") + 二 (èr, "two; second") + 代 (dài, "generation")
  - **interpretation:** The term 官二代 (guān’èrdài) refers to the children of government officials. '二代' (èrdài, "second generation") is often used in Chinese to denote the children or descendants of a certain group, and 官 (guān, "official") specifies that these are the offspring of people in official positions. The phrase carries connotations of privilege and inherited status in contemporary China.

    - Children of officials receive considerable attention in society, and they enjoy many opportunities superior to those of ordinary people.
  - 有( )些( )官( )二( )代( )不( )愿(願)背( )负(負)家( )族( )的( )期( )望( )。

### 官二代 → 官
- **definition:** official
- **pinyin:** guān
- **etymology:**
  - **type:** pictogram
  - **description:**
    - Depicts a building used as an official's residence or office ->
    - official
  - **interpretation:** The character 官 (guān, "official") originally depicted the concept of an official by representing a building associated with government officials. This pictogram conveyed the idea of someone holding an official position within such a building.

### 官二代 → 二
- **definition:** two; second
- **pinyin:** èr
- **etymology:**
  - **type:** ideogram
  - **description:**
    - Depicts two parallel strokes ->
    - represents the number two
  - **interpretation:** The character 二 (èr, "two") consists of two horizontal lines that visually represent the concept of 'two'. This method of using parallel strokes to denote numbers is a straightforward representation of quantity, similar to how 一 (yī, "one") represents 'one' and 三 (sān, "three") represents 'three'.
- **examples:**
  - 我( )有( )二( )支( )笔(筆)。
