## the real intention is not in the wine
### zuìwēng zhī yì bù zài jiǔ
---
- **definition:** the real intention is not in the wine
- **pinyin:** zuìwēng zhī yì bù zài jiǔ
- **components:**

  - 在

  - 酒

- **etymology:**
  - **type:** compound word
  - **description:** 醉 (zuì, 'intoxicated'), 翁 (wēng, 'elderly man'), 之 (zhī, 'of'), 意 (yì, 'intention; meaning'), 不 (bù, 'not'), 在 (zài, 'in; at'), 酒 (jiǔ, 'wine; alcohol')
  - **interpretation:** The phrase 醉翁之意不在酒 ('the real intention is not in the wine') originates from a famous poem by Ouyang Xiu, a Song dynasty scholar. The phrase suggests a situation where someone's apparent actions or statements have a hidden or secondary motive. Literally, it describes a scenario in which an old man (醉翁) seems to focus on drinking wine (酒), but his actual interest (意) lies elsewhere, perhaps in the companionship or the scenery. This idiom is used metaphorically in Chinese to indicate that someone's superficial actions or words conceal a different underlying intention or goal.
